# Meridian Trucks: The Accessory Buyer Is Already Here — We're Just Not Selling to Them

*Category whitespace & merchandising analysis · full-diagnostic mode · synthetic demonstration data throughout (see Appendix)*

**Why it matters:** Meridian's buyers attach accessories at the same rate as the best benchmark but spend $210 less per buyer doing it ($310 vs $520 median), and 44% of what they buy, they buy on the internet instead of from us. Closing even part of that spend-and-capture gap is worth on the order of $130+ per vehicle sold (directional: $210 spend gap × 64% attach) — with most of the fix requiring no new products.

---

## 1. The diagnosis: this is a merchandising problem, not a demand problem

The four diagnostic questions answer differently, and the pattern is what matters:

| Question | Meridian | Northstar (benchmark) | Read |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Attach** — do buyers buy anything? (attach rate, % of customers) | 64% | 66% | Normal. Demand exists. |
| **Spend** — how much, when they do? (median per buyer) | $310 | $520 | 40% below benchmark. |
| **Mix** — what do they buy? (purchase mix) | 48% commodity/protection | 29% commodity/protection | Basic. |
| **Channel** — where do they buy? (channel share) | 34% own channel, 44% internet | 52% own channel | Leaking. |

Normal attach + low spend + basic mix + high leakage is the signature of an under-merchandised catalog, not weak demand. Nearly two-thirds of Meridian buyers already walk in wanting accessories — they just buy cheap protective basics from us and everything more interesting from someone else, somewhere else. **Any recommendation built on demand generation would be spending money to create a customer we already have.** The recommendations below are therefore merchandising and assortment moves, sequenced cheapest-first.

## 2. The composition gap: we under-sell the categories that grow spend

Concentration in a handful of top SKUs is normal retail physics and is not the problem. The problem is what the mix is made of. All figures below are **purchase mix** (share of each brand's accessory purchases; sums to 100% per brand):

| Bucket | Meridian | Northstar | Copperline |
|---|---|---|---|
| Commodity / protection | **48%** | 29% | 45% |
| Visible / exterior personalization | **14%** | 31% | 16% |
| Utility / cargo / towing | 24% | 22% | 23% |
| Performance | 5% | 8% | 7% |
| Lifestyle / white-space | 2% | 5% | 2% |
| Other | 7% | 5% | 7% |

Meridian buyers put nearly half their accessory dollars into mats, liners, and guards — the high-attach, low-ceiling bucket. Northstar's buyers put 31% into visible personalization (running boards, wheels, lift, lighting, trim), the bucket that carries their $520 median spend. That 17-point personalization gap *is* the spend gap.

The offer side echoes it. On **catalog share** (share of SKUs offered — an offer-side figure, not behavioral): exterior personalization is 12% of Meridian's catalog (4 SKUs: one running board in one finish, one wheel, two trim pieces) against 38% of Northstar's (9 SKUs across boards, wheels, lift, lighting, flares, graphics). We under-offer the category and then don't merchandise the little we offer.

## 3. Benchmark against behavior, not presence — Copperline is the cautionary tale

Northstar is the benchmark because its *buyers' behavior* shifted, not because of what it stocks. Copperline proves the difference: its Speedworks performance sub-brand occupies 25% of its catalog (catalog share), yet performance is just 7% of what its buyers purchase (purchase mix), its overall mix is nearly as commodity-heavy as ours (45%), and its median spend is $350 — barely above Meridian. **Shelf presence alone does not move buyers.** Northstar's difference is that it merchandises the shift: four named packs sold in the vehicle configurator, shop-by-build journeys, accessorized trucks on every model page, and a bespoke trim program. Among brands whose customers actually buy personalization, Meridian's do not — and the mechanism the winner uses is merchandising, which is why simply ranging more SKUs (the Copperline move) is not the recommendation.

## 4. Channel leakage: 18 points of recapture headroom

All figures are **channel share** (share of accessory purchases by channel):

- Meridian captures **34%** of its buyers' accessory purchases in its own channel; **44%** goes to the internet, 17% to aftermarket stores.
- Northstar captures **52%** — an **18-point capture gap** at equal attach.

This is real, near-term volume: purchases that already happen, by our buyers, in categories we already stock — mats, tonneau covers, hitches, racks are exactly the portable, ship-anywhere categories internet sellers win on findability and checkout convenience, not product. The fix is digital merchandising (Section 6's pack-and-configurator moves double as the recapture play: a bundle priced at the pack level with guaranteed fitment, offered at point of vehicle order, is the one offer an internet parts retailer cannot match). We do not size this in dollars — that requires volume data we don't have — but 18 share points at benchmark-equal attach is the headroom.

## 5. What the buyer sees today: a parts counter, not a store

The current storefront experience (simulated audit, June 2026):

- Accessories listed **alphabetically within part-type categories**; the only navigation aid is a fitment filter.
- **No accessorized trucks anywhere** — a buyer cannot see what a Talon looks like with boards, a tonneau, and a bed tent on it.
- **No bundles, no named packs, no guidance** — 33 SKUs presented flat, the $1,249 exhaust listed with the same weight as a $45 door-edge guard.
- **The configurator sells zero accessories** at the moment of highest intent: point of vehicle order.

A motivated buyer at peak enthusiasm gets an alphabetized parts list. That — not demand — is why the mix skews to the commodity items buyers already know they need.

## 8. Pricing: not the problem. One product is.

On confirmed comparable items, Meridian prices sit in a ±10% band around peers — modestly above the discount-positioned players (mats +$10–20, wheel locks +$6–10, running boards +$50–70) and *below* peers where we compete on spec (paint protection film −$30 vs Northstar; alloy wheel −$30 vs Copperline's larger size). Price is not what's suppressing spend; mix is. **We recommend no broad pricing action.**

One flag, and it's a product-quality gap, not a pricing one: the **Meridian Sport Cat-Back Exhaust at $1,249** is a rebranded supplier unit — aluminized steel, single tip — selling at near-parity with Copperline's stainless Speedworks system ($1,149) and Northstar's stainless unit ($1,195). A similar price is hiding an inferior product. Discounting it would be the wrong fix; upgrading or replacing the product (Phase Next) is the right one.

## 9. The recommendation, sequenced

**Now — committable this quarter, today's catalog, no new products:**

1. **Launch the three packs** (Section 7) on the storefront at bundle pricing.
2. **Re-merchandise the store:** shop-by-build journeys mirroring the pack identities, accessorized Talon imagery on model pages, top sellers surfaced, guided "build your truck" flow instead of the alphabetical list.
3. **Put accessories — led by the packs — into the vehicle configurator** at point of order (the one Now item with an IT dependency; start it first).
4. **Run the recapture play on leaking categories:** pack pricing plus a fitment guarantee positions the own channel against internet parts retailers where 44% of purchases (channel share) currently go.

**Next — new capability, sequenced after the merchandising lift is captured:**

1. **Build the exterior-personalization range:** a second running-board finish, an 18–20" wheel range, a blackout badge/trim kit, a graphics program — closing the offer-side gap behind the 17-point purchase-mix gap.
2. **Fix or reposition the exhaust** (stainless, dual-tip, or exit the price point).
3. **Pilot bespoke/made-to-order** (interior trim first), following the proven Northstar program.
4. **Test the pet niche only if** a refreshed affinity read comes in at or above par — explicitly a halo play, never sized as volume.

## 10. Next step

One low-cost proof: **launch the Basecamp Pack and one accessorized-Talon build page, and instrument them for 90 days** — attach of included SKUs, bundle take rate, own-channel share on those SKUs vs baseline. In parallel, pull per-SKU sales by channel (the one dataset this analysis lacked) so the "dead long tail" can be identified from evidence before anything is hidden or delisted. Pack performance plus that pull decides how hard to press Phase Next.
